Week One

The struggle, as they say, is real. If I had to characterize my experience in one word, it would be "stressful." I didn't know what to expect, and generally try to avoid expectations in favor of anticipation, but I was nonetheless thrown somewhat for a loop.
That's not necessarily a bad thing, though. It means I've learned the reality of what I'm in for, which means I can now set my expectations accordingly. And this page should be evidence enough that I managed to grasp some programming basics as well.
The introductory materials did not disappoint in what they predicted. I did want to quit, a couple different times. But now I'm glad I didn't. The material wasn't as hard as I'd convinced myself it was. And though my page will be far from complete, or even "nice" for that matter, for demo, it is at least a beginning, and I am happy enough with that for now.
They say the scariest thing for artists and writers is a blank page. I think the same thing is true for programming. Having now put pen to paper, if you will, the hardest part is done. I look forward to learning more, the better to build on this beginning.

Week Two

In general, the second week has, for me, gone more smoothly than the first. Somehow, I felt like I had more time to do what I needed to do. That's not to say it was without stress, but I suppose that's fairly normal when learning and applying new things.
The excercise in Pseudocoding proved to be an interesting one. It is a part of the practice of programming that I never knew existed until just recently, though in retrospect it makes a lot of sense that one would try to break up a problem into component parts. I was happy to discover that this logical excercise played to my strengths as a philosophy student, and thereby did not throw me off much at all. It was still a challenge, certainly, but one which I was equipped in advance to handle.
Though it did help that I got to pick my project. I always enjoy that.
As before, I remain interested to see how new materials will allow us to build on what's already been done.
